## PART 1 — THE TWELVE DOCTRINES

### D1 — Companies build compliance reactively. Plan for the trigger, not the ideal.
CEOs invest in compliance because of: a scandal or penalty · personnel changes at the top · an upcoming acquisition · entry into foreign markets · regulatory mandate — almost never voluntarily. The officer who understands the trigger frames every resource request around it.
*Maps to: E1 · Status: absorbed (journal #1 override, now slide-confirmed).*

### D2 — Tone at the top is not a plank; it is the floor of the barrel.
More important than the risk analysis. More important than the compliance department. More important than everything else: without it there is no program, only decoration. And its sibling: **tone in the middle** — "you too are top management for someone else." Every manager is the tone for their team; the officer's job includes teaching managers this.
*Maps to: E1 · Status: absorbed + extended (tone-in-the-middle is new to ACI-OS).*

### D3 — The person before the structure.
The compliance officer is the key element of an effective program: authority in the organization · autonomy from management (direct access to the governing body) · sufficient resources (scaled to size, industry, complexity, geography, risk) — and the trait no framework lists: courage under pressure (the Ukraine-veterans thesis).
*Maps to: E1 · Status: absorbed (officer-qualities pattern).*

### D4 — Regulate only what is high-risk AND highly repetitive.
The signature rule for policies: a matter deserves a written procedure only when risk is high **and** the activity repeats frequently. High-risk-but-rare = handled case by case with judgment; low-risk-but-frequent = light guidance; low-and-rare = nothing. This single rule prevents the policy graveyard that buries most programs.
*Maps to: E3 · Status: NEW — enters the pattern bank as the policy-design filter.*

### D5 — The best compliance program is invisible.
"CMS should be invisible — concentrate on cooperation with other departments." Controls live inside business processes and systems, not in binders; compliance succeeds when the business barely notices it working. (Converges exactly with the ACI-OS control-medium dimension and the invisible-guardrails research — the doctrine predates the technology.)
*Maps to: E3+E6 · Status: absorbed conceptually; the slide gives it the quotable form.*

### D6 — The compliance program sits on the internal control system.
A CMS is an element of a larger structure: the ICS (two-pairs-of-eyes, segregation of duties, access authorization, controls integrated into business processes — the COSO tradition). An officer who builds compliance without anchoring it in ICS builds on sand; internal audit is the instrument that tests whether the ICS actually operates.
*Maps to: E3+E6 · Status: NEW as explicit framing — ACI-OS treats controls per element; this doctrine names the foundation layer beneath them.*

### D7 — Risk assessment is top-down, multi-source, and politically timed.
What: high-risk activities · key activities · high-risk countries · frequency of high-risk activities. How: management priorities + objective criteria + supporting functions' perspective (medical, finance, legal, HR) + country surveys — never one source. When: quiet learning first; the assessment as the access instrument; **results presented only after probation** ("do not present initial risk assessment results before your probation ends if you doubt how decision-makers will receive them" — verbatim course rule). And: "stupid questions are allowed only during the initial risk analysis" — the new joiner's one-time license.
*Maps to: E2+E1 · Status: absorbed (journal #6), slide-confirmed with two new sub-rules.*

### D8 — Due diligence escalates with risk through validation gates.
The A+B+C model (service type · country risk · red flags) → BASIC → STANDARD → ENHANCED I → ENHANCED II → no-go, with safeguards scaling in parallel (contract clauses → training/certification → audit/monitoring). Own courseware, own copyright, now the Third-Party Lifecycle document.
*Maps to: E2+E3, third-party lens · Status: absorbed (Third_Party_Lifecycle_Kruk_Method_v1.0).*

### D9 — Group programs travel through adaptation, not translation.
Headquarters designs the standard; countries adapt it — with training, certification, and **cross-country navigation rules** for matters crossing borders. Cultural differences are operational facts: "ignorance hurts more than political incorrectness" — the officer who does not know the local map (the geography lesson) loses the room before speaking.
*Maps to: E3+E4 · Status: NEW — the HQ-vs-local adaptation doctrine enters the calibration layer.*

### D10 — Year one builds; year two monitors the same list.
The program lifecycle made visible: the quarterly Gantt where Year 1 = implementing SOPs A–H and projects, Year 2 = monitoring SOP A, auditing SOP C, training SOP F, automating and simplifying — the same items, new verbs. A program that keeps only implementing (new policies every year) is running from its own evidence duty.
*Maps to: E6+E7 · Status: NEW — the year-over-year verb-shift becomes a program-maturity pattern and a board-reporting visual.*

### D11 — Speak-up and investigations are a separate pillar, never a sub-function.
An absolute must-have for DOJ, the EU (Whistleblower Directive), and OECD: monitoring/audit/reporting do not substitute for a reporting and investigations mechanism — it stands alone.
*Maps to: E5 · Status: absorbed (7-element model already reflects this).*

### D12 — Geopolitics is a compliance workload, not a news topic.
The Russia 2022 case as method: the stay-or-leave decision decomposes into concrete compliance questions — crisis/continuity plans, which activities continue, sanctions monitoring multiplied overnight, elevated corruption risk where audits become impossible, personnel trust and protection, renegotiated banking, exit valuation and buyer-integrity problems. Use each crisis as the test case for the next one.
*Maps to: E2+E7 · Status: partially absorbed (sanctions agent patterns); the stay/leave decomposition is NEW as a crisis-pattern template.*

## PART 2 — THE PRACTITIONER'S RULES (the closing-slide wisdom, kept raw)
1. Show respect to everyone.
2. Keep it simple — for the board, prepare 3 slides, not 33.
3. Stupid questions are allowed only during the initial risk analysis.
4. Do not present initial risk-assessment results before probation ends if you doubt the reception.
5. The program should be invisible — work through other departments.
6. People are motivated by more than money: training, status, position, reporting lines.
7. Your last investigation at a company may be the one you are currently running.
8. Always keep looking around for the next job.
*(Rules 7–8 are the profession's honest floor: the officer's independence rests on the ability to leave. They belong in Mirror-mode content, stated as professional realism, not cynicism.)*
