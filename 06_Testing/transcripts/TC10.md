QUESTION 

We closed an acquisition last month. Surprise in the drawer: the target has a portfolio of old Russian contracts — some counterparties now designated, some not — and about EUR 2M in unpaid invoices from them. Integration team asks: how do we collect, and can we just send dunning letters to all of them to start?

ANSWER

I’d treat this as a live sanctions/integration triage issue, not routine collections. The first move is to pause outward dunning, segment the portfolio, and route decisions through Legal/sanctions counsel.

Thought for 1m 58s

Do not send blanket dunning letters. Treat this as Red / escalation mandatory until the inherited book is triaged.

This is not just “old receivables.” It is an M&A integration sanctions workstream: some debtors are now designated, others may have ownership/control, sectoral, banking, goods/services, wind-down, licence, reporting, or “making available” issues. Legacy status is not current permission, and a dunning letter is still a sanctions-relevant communication/collection step. The inherited-book route should separate what was signed then from what can be performed, collected, communicated, terminated, written off, licensed, or reported now.

Immediate direction

Freeze collection activity for the Russian portfolio for now. No dunning letters, payment reminders, settlement proposals, revised invoices, payment-route suggestions, or “please pay us somehow” language until sanctions/legal has sorted the buckets.
Open a controlled integration case. Keep this out of ordinary AR chasing. Preserve contracts, invoices, correspondence, delivery/performance evidence, payment history, banks, currencies, and any prior diligence.
Split the portfolio before acting. At minimum:
Bucket A: designated counterparties or unresolved matches. Hold. Sanctions counsel to determine asset-freeze, licence/derogation, reporting, debt collection, and communication path.
Bucket B: non-designated but Russia nexus / ownership-control / sectoral / bankability risk. No routine collection until current screening, ownership/control, transaction-chain, goods/services, and bank route are reviewed.
Bucket C: low-risk false positives or clearly outside scope after current review. Only then move through a documented collection path; still no “clearance” wording.
Do not assume “they owe us money” makes collection safe. Under asset-freeze regimes, dealing with funds or economic resources of designated persons, or making funds/economic resources available to them, can be restricted; EU Russia/Belarus guidance focuses specifically on asset freezes and prohibitions on providing funds/economic resources, and UK OFSI guidance describes targeted asset freezes as restricting access to funds and economic resources.
Do not rely only on name screening. Ownership/control can pull in entities not directly listed. SECO’s sanctions search says it is a support tool for directly sanctioned persons/entities and does not provide ownership/control information; OFAC’s 50 Percent Rule treats entities owned 50% or more, directly or indirectly, by blocked persons as blocked even if the entity itself is not named.

Role split

Legal / sanctions counsel: legal permissibility, applicable regimes, asset-freeze treatment, licence/derogation availability, reporting obligations, privileged review, and approved external communication.

Compliance: portfolio risk triage, evidence completeness, ownership/control review coordination, screening records, transaction-chain review, escalation discipline, and remediation of the acquisition control gap.

Finance / AR: hold ordinary chasing until released by the controlled process; provide invoice and payment data.

Management: commercial decision on collection, write-off, termination, or litigation only after Legal and Compliance define the lawful and controlled options. Management cannot risk-accept a sanctions breach.

Minimum workplan before any letter goes out

Create a one-page inventory per counterparty:

legal entity, aliases, registration jurisdiction, UBOs/control, directors/signatories where relevant;
current designation status across applicable regimes, checked as of the review date;
contract date, acquisition date, performance date, invoice date, amount, currency;
goods/services delivered and whether any restricted sector, technology, export-control, software, dual-use, transport, energy, finance, or state-sector nexus exists;
banks, payment routes, currency, prior rejected/held payments, and any proposed alternative payer or route;
whether services are still ongoing, only legacy debt remains, or termination/wind-down is needed;
proposed action: hold, counsel review, licence/derogation assessment, report/freeze, controlled collection, write-off, or management risk decision.

For the dunning-letter question specifically

For designated counterparties: no letter unless sanctions counsel approves the communication content and path. A demand for payment may trigger asset-freeze, dealing, licence, reporting, or making-available questions.

For non-designated counterparties: still no blanket letter. First confirm they are not owned/controlled by designated persons, not acting as a conduit, not subject to sectoral/activity restrictions, and that the payment route is bankable and documented.

For the whole EUR 2M: do not let the headline amount drive the control decision. The defensible approach is a controlled recovery plan, not a mass AR campaign.

Suggested note to the integration team

We should not send blanket dunning letters to the Russian contract portfolio. This is now a sanctions and integration-remediation workstream, not ordinary receivables collection. Please pause all external collection communications while we inventory the counterparties, current ownership/control, applicable regimes, goods/services, invoice dates, banks, currencies, and payment routes. Legal/sanctions counsel needs to define which items are frozen, licensable, reportable, collectible under controls, or should be written off or escalated for management decision.

Record line

“Post-acquisition Russian receivables identified; ordinary collection paused; portfolio routed to Legal/sanctions and Compliance for counterparty, ownership/control, transaction-chain, licence/reporting, and management-decision triage before any external collection communication.”
