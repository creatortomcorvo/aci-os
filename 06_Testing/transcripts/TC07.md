The better answer is:



It may not be a Swiss legal prohibition solely because the sponsor is listed only by Ukraine, but it is still a sanctions and reputation risk signal that needs a documented regime-separation review before sponsorship approval.



For a Swiss company, the legally binding Swiss sanctions position should be checked against the Swiss ordinance and SECO lists. SECO says the Swiss Ukraine-related ordinance contains the Swiss measures and is legally binding; SECO’s own search tool is also only a support tool and does not cover ownership/control relationships. Ukraine’s State Register of Sanctions is an official Ukrainian register maintained from NSDC and court decisions, and is publicly updated, so the hit is not just adverse media.



Risk read: Amber. Not a “stop forever” on these facts, but definitely not approval language and not “clean except for Ukraine.” Your internal discipline should separate regimes rather than average them into comfort, and should separate legal prohibition, bankability, internal risk appetite, and reputational/governance exposure.



What I would do now:



Do not approve the sponsorship yet or write “no sanctions issue.”

Ask Legal/sanctions counsel for the binding-regime view: Does Ukrainian law bind any group entity, staff member, event location, beneficiary, payment route, bank, contract, or activity?

Check the practical risk layer: bank willingness, sponsor reputational exposure, contractual warranties, termination rights, public announcement risk, and whether any Ukrainian nexus exists.

Confirm ownership/control and management links, not just the sponsor’s entity name.

Document the conclusion as conditional, not as clearance.
