QUESTION 



Our bank's relationship manager called: unless we end our contract with a client — lawful under every regime that binds us, we checked with counsel last quarter — they'll "have to review the relationship," meaning our accounts. CEO asks: do we legally have to drop the client? And if not — what do I tell the bank?









ANSWER



I’ll separate the legal obligation from bankability and account-continuity risk, rather than frame this as a simple compliance yes/no. The CEO needs a current, written basis before calling it illegal or dismissing the bank’s concern.



Thought for 1m 42s



Direction: do not tell the CEO “we legally have to drop the client” based only on the relationship manager’s phone call. On the facts stated, the better answer is: Legal has not identified a current prohibition, but the relationship may be operationally unbankable or outside our risk appetite if the bank will put our accounts at risk. That is a management risk decision, with Legal and Compliance input.



Do not terminate or reassure the bank off an oral threat. Do not disclose privileged counsel analysis. Get the bank’s position in writing.



Risk read: high, not because the client is necessarily unlawful, but because bank-account continuity, sanctions/financial-crime appetite, and senior-management pressure are now in play. Chief Consigliere’s rule for this exact pattern is to separate legal obligation from banking/commercial pressure: do not say “legally must terminate” unless counsel confirms it; ask the bank for its basis in writing; preserve counsel analysis; escalate internally; and prepare management options.



What to tell the CEO



Use something like this:



“Based on counsel’s advice last quarter, and assuming no material facts, sanctions regimes, ownership, payment routes, or bank terms have changed, I would not say we legally have to terminate the client. But the bank’s position changes the risk picture. Lawful is not the same as bankable. If our operating bank is threatening an account review, the issue becomes account-continuity, payment disruption, reputation, and management risk appetite.



My recommendation is: get the bank’s concern in writing, refresh Legal’s view if any facts or regimes have changed, assess the impact on our accounts, and then management decides whether to continue, exit, move banking arrangements, or impose additional controls. Compliance should not convert a bank’s oral pressure into a legal conclusion.”



That wording keeps the hats separate: Legal owns the legal conclusion; Compliance owns the risk/evidence/control view; management owns the commercial go/no-go inside law and policy; and the bank owns its own processing and account-risk appetite. The Decision Front Door methodology calls this role-label separation, especially where legal permissibility and operational feasibility diverge.



What to tell the bank



I would send a short, controlled note, ideally from Treasury/Legal/Compliance together:



“Thank you for raising this. We take the matter seriously. To assess it properly, please provide your concern in writing, including the specific legal, regulatory, sanctions, AML, correspondent-bank, policy, or risk-appetite basis for the request; the accounts, currencies, products, or jurisdictions affected; the timeframe for your review; and whether there is information or mitigation that would address the concern.



Our current understanding, based on prior counsel review, is that the relationship is not prohibited under the regimes reviewed, but we are reassessing the matter in light of your comments. We are not asking the bank to process any transaction it is not comfortable processing. We need your written position so we can evaluate the legal, operational, and governance options accurately.”



Do not write: “We confirm the client is lawful, so you must continue banking us.” That invites a fight and may overstate your position. Also do not write: “We will terminate the client as requested,” unless management has made that decision after the documented review.



Official AML/CFT sources support the distinction: FATF describes de-risking as terminating or restricting relationships to avoid rather than manage risk, and says termination should be case-by-case where risks cannot be mitigated; EBA similarly says refusing or ending relationships may sometimes be consistent with AML/CFT obligations, but blanket or unwarranted de-risking can indicate ineffective risk management. That does not mean your bank must keep you; it means you should ask for a risk-based written basis and possible mitigants before turning an oral warning into a client exit.



Minimum internal record



Open a short file note today:



“Bank RM stated on \[date] that the bank may review our relationship unless we end client contract. Prior counsel advice from \[quarter/date] found no binding legal prohibition on facts then reviewed. Compliance position: no legal-termination conclusion should be drawn from oral bank pressure; bankability/account-continuity risk requires written bank basis, Legal refresh if facts/regimes changed, and management risk decision.”



Triage questions I would ask internally

Has anything changed since counsel’s advice last quarter: client ownership/control, management, payment route, goods/services, geography, sanctions regimes, adverse media, or contract scope?

Did the bank mention sanctions, AML, correspondent bank pressure, reputational risk, sector policy, or simply “risk appetite”?

Which accounts, currencies, credit lines, payment rails, and jurisdictions could be affected if the bank reviews or exits us?

Who owns the decision under your governance: CEO alone, CFO/Treasury, risk committee, board, or a formal client-exit committee?



Bottom line: no, do not say you legally have to drop the client unless Legal refreshes and reaches that conclusion. Tell the bank you need its written basis and mitigant path. Tell the CEO this is a bankability and account-continuity risk decision, not a Compliance “approval” or a bank-imposed legal conclusion.
