## D20 — The Six-Stage Compliance Adoption Curve, calibrated by national culture (the geography lesson, full form)

**The instrument.** Any global procedure rollout passes through six observable stages, and organizational behavior at each stage is predictable by country culture:
1. **Adoption (localization)** — cooperation when adapting the global procedure locally
2. **Pre-implementation training** — reaction and engagement during training, before go-live
3. **Day-1 compliance** — adherence with no monitoring and no enforcement yet
4. **Six-month compliance** — adherence still without monitoring (the decay test)
5. **Monitoring announced** — behavior when oversight becomes visible
6. **After first disciplinary action** — behavior once consequences are demonstrated

**The doctrine:** compliance does not fail at rollout; it decays at stages 3–4 and is rescued at stages 5–6 — and the *shape* of that curve is cultural. The officer who knows the local curve plans monitoring and enforcement timing per country instead of being surprised by the decay. This is the operational expansion of D9 ("ignorance hurts more than political incorrectness") — the anonymous 5-type matrix in the CMS deck, here developed into full named profiles.

**The twelve compliance-logic formulas (each country's curve in one sentence):**
| Country | Compliance logic |
|---|---|
| France | "If it is intellectually sound and well-argued, it will be followed" — debate is respect; why before how |
| Germany | "If it is structured, logical, and fair, it will be followed" — rules internalized as order; concerns raised formally, never via silent non-compliance |
| United States | "If it is measured and linked to performance, it will be followed" — compliance is transactional; decays fast if unenforced, snaps back when tied to KPIs |
| United Kingdom | "If it is fair, it is followed" — selective obedience; quiet "Britishizing" of impractical rules; proportionality expected in enforcement |
| Switzerland | "Rules are part of professional integrity; they reflect order, not constraint" — Day-1 compliance high without any monitoring; misalignment flagged through orderly channels |
| Poland | "Follow rules when rational, leadership-backed, or monitored — while watching for inefficiency" — system navigation is a national skill; supervisor influence decisive |
| Russia | "What matters is who enforces the rules and what the real consequences are" — formal acceptance, informal reinterpretation; the system vs. the real world |
| China | "Rules are followed when backed by authority, monitored, and preserving harmony" — face, harmony, relationships govern; adaptation happens privately, never in open debate |
| Saudi Arabia | "We follow rules when respected leaders endorse them and honor is at stake" — loyalty to leaders over systems; shame is the enforcement mechanism |
| Argentina | "Rules work if the people implementing them are trustworthy and flexible" — trust the messenger, not the message; relational anchoring or decay |
| Algeria | "We respect rules when fairly enforced, culturally sensitive, and linked to real consequences" — formal systems coexist with informal practice |
| Turkey | Leader-endorsed pragmatism — rules gain traction through trusted authority figures; public discipline works but can stifle initiative |

**Each profile in the source deck carries:** key cultural traits → per-stage likely reaction + cultural insight → summary table → compliance-logic formula → risk factors and strengths. The structure itself is reusable: any new country = traits + six-stage predictions + one-line logic.

## D21 — Culture is diagnosable: Schein's three levels mapped to the compliance program

**The definition (core, quotable):** an organizational culture of integrity is a collective environment where ethical behavior, honesty, and accountability are *intrinsically valued, not merely enforced* — operating through (1) shared ethical norms guiding decisions, (2) leadership modeling of moral principles, (3) accountability systems ensuring transparency and fairness, (4) **alignment between stated values and actual practice across all layers**.

**The diagnostic:** Schein's three levels of culture, mapped to the compliance program:
| Schein level | What it looks like in compliance | Program elements it carries |
|---|---|---|
| **Artifacts** (visible) | Public code of conduct, transparent reporting channels, recognition of ethical behavior, even office symbols and jokes | Risk assessment, policies, training, reporting, third-party mgmt, resources |
| **Espoused values** (declared) | Mission statements putting ethics before profit; ethics training programs; leadership commitments | Leadership commitment, resource allocation, consequence management, improvement |
| **Underlying assumptions** (unspoken, decisive) | Employees report misconduct instinctively, without fear; leaders choose long-term ethics over short-term gains | Psychological safety, shared accountability, compliance as non-negotiable |

**The doctrine:** programs live at the artifact level; culture lives at the assumption level; the gap between them is where scandals grow. An officer can *diagnose* culture by testing alignment: do the artifacts and the espoused values predict what people actually assume? (Ties to D2 — tone is the floor — and gives it a measurement method.)
*Integration: culture-assessment pattern (M4) — "assess our culture" questions get the three-level diagnostic, not a survey recommendation. Element: E1.*
